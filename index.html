How Called Out works

From "they're speaking" to "point scored" — in under a second.

Called Out runs a structured debate with a live transcript and an AI fact-checker sitting on top of every sentence. When the fact-check engine (Groq or Gemini) flags a factual claim, the screen flashes green or red and the points post automatically. Here's the full pipeline.

1 Setup

Pick one of seven debate formats (Casual, Public Forum, Lincoln-Douglas, Policy, Parliamentary, Mock Trial). Competitive formats auto-configure a fixed phase sequence (e.g. Parliamentary runs PMC → LOC → MG → MO → LOR → PMR with authentic time limits). Casual mode lets you pick rounds, seconds-per-turn, and whether sides alternate.

Set a topic (preset or custom), name the debaters, choose your speech-recognition language, configure point values, and decide whether you want the AI judge to weigh in at the end.

2 Live speech capture

During a turn, the browser's Web Speech API (Chrome / Edge) streams audio through your microphone and returns a rolling transcript. Interim text appears faded and locks in when the recognizer is confident. The mic light on the subtitles panel tells you we're actively listening.

Every final chunk — roughly a sentence — is handed off to the fact-check pipeline.

3 Claim detection & fact-check

Each chunk is split into candidate sentences and queued against the active provider (Groq by default, Gemini optional) with a strict, JSON-only prompt asking two things:

  1. Is there a verifiable factual claim? (Opinions, questions, filler, and predictions are skipped.)
  2. Is that claim true or false based on well-established public knowledge? Plus a confidence score.

To keep things responsive and avoid API thrash, requests run through a queue with a 1.5-second cooldown, repeat sentences inside a turn are de-duplicated, and nothing fires during cross-fire phases (where we can't attribute who said what).

4 Instant feedback

When the fact-check provider returns:

  • Verified (confidence ≥ 0.7) — screen flashes green, a short high tone plays, and the current speaker gets +fact.
  • False (confidence ≥ 0.7) — screen flashes red, a low tone plays, and the current speaker loses −wrong.
  • Unclear or non-claim — a banner briefly shows the verdict, but no points change.

A banner near the top shows the paraphrased claim, the model's one-sentence explanation, and which side was scored. Every automatic event is tagged · auto in the undo tray, so you can reverse anything with one click.

5 Manual scoring & fallacies

You're still in charge. Hit +fact / −wrong on either debater panel for anything the auto-checker missed. The −fallacy button opens a picker for 16 common logical fallacies (Ad Hominem, Straw Man, Slippery Slope, etc.) — each one docks the fallacy penalty and appends a tag to the transcript.

You can also highlight any phrase in the live subtitles to pop up a one-click "Fact-check this" shortcut, which runs the same prompt on demand against whichever provider you've chosen.

6 End of debate

When the last phase ends (or you hit "End debate"), you land on the Results screen: final scores, the tally of facts / wrong claims / fallacies per side, a highlight reel of every tagged moment, and the full timestamped transcript ready to export as plain text.

If you enabled AI Judging, the same provider also issues a written verdict — either by points (summarizing the tally) or by substance (reading the transcript and picking whoever made the stronger argument).

· Keys & privacy

Your API keys live in .env (source of truth, git-ignored) and their browser-readable mirror config.js (also git-ignored). Nothing is sent to any server we control — your audio stays in your browser via the Web Speech API, and transcript chunks go directly to the provider's endpoint (Groq or Google) using your own key. If the key is missing or rejected, auto fact-check silently disables and the app keeps working in fully-manual mode.

You can swap providers on the Setup screen. Auto fact-check supports Groq and Gemini. The end-of-debate AI judge supports Groq, Gemini, Anthropic Claude, and OpenAI.

· When to overrule the AI

No LLM is infallible — especially on statistics, very recent events, or claims that hinge on precise wording. The confidence threshold (0.7) is tuned so it only auto-scores when it's reasonably sure, but you should still watch the banner and undo any call that looks off.
